# Goodbye AI.

Slowing raising the red, ceramic mug to my blonde-’stashed lips, I breathe in the morning scent: a fresh cup of black coffee. The cool spring breeze of the north country brushes against my skin through the open window. I can hear the morning: the birds singing in the trees, the brustling of amies (thousands strong) of leaves in response to the wind, and the occasional drip of the last night’s rain falling from the trees. Half-wakened, I slowly glide my finger tips across the black keys of my Macbook, thinking what to jot down digitally. It is a new day, the morning of a workday.

I didn’t choose to wake up this early (5:30 am), but here I am…my body betraying me for better or for worse. The silver lining: having the fleeting moment to drink in the soothing sounds of the morning aura, a pleasant nod by the overcast sky. This is nature seemingly woken up to greet my weighted heart of a semi-depressed engineer. I can’t help it but I find myself pondering some of the latest advancements as of late: the Google I/O 2026 event, the latest mandates at work, the latest outcome of the (might I mention completely asenine) OpenAI versus Elon court case. The world feels fucked right now. Well, at least it seems like it is headed in that direction.

During my daily meditations, I noticed a change of self, a new development within the inner portions of my psyche. For the longest time, I was excited about technology and the developments around technology. My parents could probably tell you that I have been pretty obsessed since early highschool, turning that obsession into a full-blown career. Building software was a dream, it was exciting, it was fun. The challenge that came from figuring things out and exercising the mind to solve problems and debug source code… that made work enjoyable.

The landscape has changed.

It would not be such an issue if the new technology and tooling was presented in a way that was deemed helpful as an additive side-car; AI as an assistant to the already well-oiled machine: the IDE that we software engineers use everyday (well, most of us… maybe not the members of the VIM cult). This is not how it is presented though. What started off as a “useful assistant” has slowly morphed into a completely different beast. The whole trend of “agentic coding” (fancy language for the less-professional term: “vibe coding”) has penetrated the enterprise industry of software development. It is everywhere and is unavoidable.

Again, this would not be a problem if it was presented in a way as a “helpful side-tool” to accelerate work. On paper and in emails, the AI tools are written down as helpful assistants for developing software faster and more efficiently. To the contrary of that narrative however, through the levels of management… these tools are stated/presented as non-optional, required, and mandated to be shoe-horned in the workflows everyday for all software engineers. This is no longer a “helpful assistant” but a “sublte” (or not so subtle, frankly) dehumanizing of the creative ingenuity of traditional software development. Frankly, I don't fucking appreciate it.

This forcing of AI down my throat, it leaves a bad taste in my mouth. Anywhere I look in the tech industry, at work, online, in the products used everyday… it is one thing if this was optional, but most of the time it is required to be interacted with. This is especially prevalent at work where virtually every email coming from upper management has some blurb about "AI this" or "AI that.” This, in whole, has added to the already somewhat critical feeling that I had concerning AI tools… now turning those feelings into a more negative form. In general, this all has started to pose a negative threat to my mental health and as a drain on my motivation to go above and beyond at work and within my own personal life.

That brings me then to the actual point of writing this blog entry: I uninstalled Claude from my laptop. Gone. Deleted. Goodbye. Why? It just is much better for my mental health that I do. The constant forcing of AI into everything all around me… and especially at work has got me feeling bad. I used to be proud of what. do at work, of where I had gotten, and what I had achieved. I understand that this whole “AI revolution” can be a huge productivity leap, that we can accelerate the work software engineering teams do around the world to be faster than ever. However, what I do not see in the industry is this: at what cost does forcing these tools into the lives of everyone cost us? What are we losing by having a machine actually doing the majority of the work? Not to mention the highly questionable origins of AI as a whole.

For me, I had to stop using the tooling in my personal life, at least in terms of paying for a standalone service and using the Claude apps on my mac and iPhone. It was and is costing me my motivation, my enthusiasm. It is costing me my passion for technology. I blame the current AI-obsessed fetish coming out of Silicon Valley and San Francisco for this change. Like I had read previously in the multitude of books on Stoicism and more recently Wabi Sabi… we are only in control of how we respond to the change around us, we are not in control of the change or even many things in our own lives. That being said, the best I can do to deal with this frustration over the forced adoption of AI is to simply remove it within my personal life in order to help preserve a better, more healthy mental health. AI is merely a tool, regaardless of what many people seem to treat it as more than that. In my case, I plan to treat it as a simple work tool and refrain from using it outside of work.

There are exceptions, however, for AI tooling that comes built into applications, systems, etc that I use that do not shove that tooling in your face though. I do find some usecases useful, I just am so fed up with this forced, mandated use of the AI tooling everywhere. It would be so much more productive for this technology to be optional.
